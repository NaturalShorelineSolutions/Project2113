Protecting Significant Natural Heritage on Two Kawartha Lake Shorelines

Project Description

There has been a significant gap in current, reliable landscape and parcel-based information that has been required for the planning and implementation of protection efforts of ecosystems around Clear and Stoney Lake, Ontario.

As a result, the progress to protect and conserve biologically important land by our clients, the Environment Council for Clear, Ston(e)y, and White Lakes and the Kawartha Land Trust, has been impeded.

The current condition of the shoreline and shorefront land was assessed to determine where natural heritage still existed and where it had been destroyed due to the alteration of the area. This was done through a shoreline inventory and an analysis of the shoreline combined with the surrounding wetlands, watercourses, ecosystem connectivity features, and building density.

This project has provided our clients with up-to-date data and comprehensive mapping that will significantly aid in the protection efforts for Clear and Stoney Lakes as well as future studies in the region.

Methodology

To meet the objectives set by the clients, our methodology was split into data acquisition, shoreline inventory, and parcel analysis.

Data Acquisition

4 criteria were identified as important factors to be assessed for parcel suitability. These four criteria included building density, natural heritage features (also known as baseline connectivity features), habitat features (in particular the presence of wetlands and watercourses) and shoreline features/metrics. Data for the first 3 criteria were acquired through publicly available data created by the Ministry of Natural Resources and Forestry, and through connections with municipal counties such as Peterborough County. Following data acquisition, data was verified using 2018 SCOOP imagery (NAME) provided by Peterborough County.

Shoreline Inventory

Using Best Practices developed by the Ministry of Natural Resources and Forestry (DATE), a classification methodology was created for the Stoney Lake and Clear Lake shorelines. Digitization of 9.8km of the lake were initially conducted through the use of 2018 SCOOP imagery. To verify the accuracy of data collected using 2018 SCOOP imagery and to collect ground-truthed data field methodology was created and carried out by KLT staff. Using field collected data the initial 9.8km of shoreline data was validated, and an additional 12.5km of shoreline data was collected and digitized from.

Map of shoreline digitized to-date.

Parcel Analysis

Following data acquisition, a parcel analysis was preformed to identify suitable parcels for conservation. Parcels were assessed for suitability for each criterion as high, medium, or low suitability. The results of the suitability for each criterion were then combined to create the final surface featured.

Solution

The final rsults of our project, obtained through the methods above, were summarized and displayed in an interactive web application, created using ArcGIS Experience Builder (a system for building configurable applications using premade widgets).

Project Overview

Experience Builder Application: Project Overview page.

The first of four pages of our application, Project Overview outlines the goals of the project and the organizations involved, and it introduces users to Stoney and Clear Lakes by way of the map.

Shoreline Digitization

Experience Builder Application: Shoreline Digitization page

Shoreline Digitization allows users to learn about and explore our digitization methodology and view the different shoreline features that have been classified during the project.

Parcel Analysis

Experience Builder Application: Parcel Analysis page.

Parcel Analysis provides users with a description of our methodology and thought-processes behind our analysis of parcel suitability. It also allows users to interact with the data by viewing the parcels and their underlying information, including individual criteria rankings, relevant landscape features, and the state of the shoreline. Users may control the visible map features by turning layers on or off using the list of buttons on the right.

Next Steps

Experience Builder Application: Next Steps page.

Finally, the Next Steps page outlines where we left off in digitizing the shoreline, what should be done in future projects, and the usefulness of our analysis.
